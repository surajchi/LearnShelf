  • Distinguish the three states of data: in use, in transit, and at rest.
  • Explain cloud-based service models (SaaS, PaaS, IaaS) and the shared responsibility model as it applies to cloud security.

Risk, Revisited: Likelihood × Impact

Course II introduced risk as the intersection of threat and vulnerability. This module gives that relationship a working formula you'll use constantly for the rest of the certificate.

📖DefinitionDEF-401

Risk (as a calculation): Likelihood × Impact = Risk. Security professionals primarily focus on the likelihood side of the equation, since the business impact of a negative event depends heavily on the specific asset and situation.

Explain Like I'm Five — Driving to Work

You risk being late to work every time you drive. A flat tire (a threat exploiting a worn tire — a vulnerability) makes that late arrival more likely. And the impact could be serious — losing your job. Multiply how likely something is by how bad it would be, and you get a working sense of the actual risk. Businesses calculate security risk exactly the same way.

Threat Categories vs. Vulnerability Categories
ThreatsVulnerabilities
Intentional — a malicious hacker exploiting a misconfigured applicationTechnical — misconfigured software granting unauthorized access
Unintentional — an employee holding a door open for an unknown personHuman — a forgetful employee losing their access card
✅Best PracticeBP-401

Calculating risk this way helps organizations do four specific things: prevent costly and disruptive events, identify system/process improvements, determine which risks can be tolerated, and prioritize which critical assets need attention first.

Asset Management & Classification

📖DefinitionDEF-402

Asset management: The process of tracking assets and the risks that affect them — forming the foundation of all security plans.

Asset inventory: A comprehensive catalog of assets requiring protection, preventing loss or misplacement and enabling efficient resource allocation.

Asset classification: The practice of categorizing assets based on their sensitivity and importance to an organization.

The Four Common Asset Classification Levels
LevelMeaningExample
RestrictedHighest level — incredibly sensitive, "need-to-know" informationIntellectual property, sensitive personal data
ConfidentialDisclosure could lead to significant negative impact on the organizationUnreleased financial results
Internal-OnlyAvailable to employees and business partners, not external partiesInternal memos, org charts
PublicLowest level — no negative consequences if releasedMarketing brochures, press releases
❗ImportantIMP-401

Government organizations sometimes label their most sensitive assets "confidential" instead of "restricted" — the exact naming scheme varies by organization, but the underlying logic (four tiers by sensitivity) is nearly universal. Also note: identifying an asset's owner is sometimes the hardest classification characteristic to pin down — e.g., who "owns" personal photos stored on a company-issued laptop?

Data States: In Use, In Transit, At Rest

📖DefinitionDEF-403

Data in use: Data actively being accessed by one or more users — reading an email, editing a document.

Data in transit: Data moving from one point to another — sending an email or file over the internet.

Data at rest: Data not currently being accessed, typically stored on a physical device — files on a laptop or server.

⚠️WarningWRN-401

Our understanding of "data at rest" is shifting. With cloud storage constantly syncing, replicating, and backing up in the background, data may not be truly "at rest" even when a device itself isn't actively in use. Treat this as an evolving vulnerability surface, not a settled category.

Cloud-Based Services & Cloud Security

Cloud Service Models Revisited
ModelDefinitionExamples
SaaSFront-end applications users access via browser; provider hosts/manages/maintains all back-end systemsGmail, Slack, Zoom
PaaSBack-end development tools clients access online to build their own appsGoogle App Engine, Heroku, VMware Cloud Foundry
IaaSRemote access to back-end systems (servers, storage, networking), licensed as neededGoogle Cloud Platform, Microsoft Azure
❗Important — Shared Responsibility, RevisitedIMP-402

Clients are commonly responsible for securing anything directly within their control: identity and access management, resource configuration, and data handling. The exact split of responsibility varies by service model — a PaaS client secures the apps they build; the provider secures the underlying servers shared across all their customers.

Cloud Security Challenges
ChallengeWhy It Happens
MisconfigurationCustomers use out-of-the-box configurations that don't address their specific security objectives
Cloud-native breachesMore likely to occur specifically due to misconfigured services
Difficult access monitoringDepends heavily on client setup and level of service purchased
Regulatory complianceEspecially relevant in industries bound by HIPAA, PCI DSS, or GDPR
🏢Industry InsightIND-401

Cloud security is consistently ranked among the most in-demand cybersecurity skills by labor market analytics firms — a direct consequence of how quickly organizations have migrated critical infrastructure to the cloud without always building matching in-house security expertise.

Security Plans: Policies, Standards & Procedures

📖DefinitionDEF-404

Policies: A set of rules aimed at reducing risk and protecting information, defining the scope, objectives, and limitations of security measures.

Standards: References informing how policies should be implemented — e.g., NIST SP 800-63B specifying password length requirements.

Procedures: Step-by-step instructions for performing specific security tasks, promoting accountability, consistency, and efficiency.

Explain Like I'm Five — The School, Again

A school's policy might say "protect student records." The standard specifies exactly how strong the lock on the records room must be. The procedure is the literal step-by-step instructions taped to the wall for exactly how staff should check a record out and back in. Policy → standard → procedure gets progressively more concrete.

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework: Core, Tiers, Profiles

📖DefinitionDEF-405

NIST CSF: A voluntary framework of standards, guidelines, and best practices to manage cybersecurity risk, originally released in 2014 to protect U.S. critical infrastructure and since adapted for public and private sector use of any size.

NIST CSF Components in Detail
ComponentFunction
CoreA security checklist of five functions: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover
TiersMeasure the sophistication of a cybersecurity program on a scale of 1 (limited controls) to 4 (adaptive, most sophisticated)
ProfilesPre-made templates tailored to specific risks, used as a baseline or to compare current posture against an industry standard
✅Best Practice — CISA's CSF Implementation StepsBP-402

The U.S. Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) recommends four steps for implementing the CSF:

  1. Create a current profile outlining your business's specific security needs.
  2. Perform a risk assessment to identify which operations meet business/regulatory standards.
  3. Analyze and prioritize existing gaps that place assets at risk.
  4. Implement a plan of action to achieve organizational goals.
❗ImportantIMP-403

Regulations must be followed; frameworks are resources you choose to use. The NIST CSF is voluntary — but many businesses adopt it specifically because it helps them achieve compliance with regulations that are mandatory, like GDPR or HIPAA.

Hands-On Labs & Practical Exercises

Lab 1.1 — Risk Calculation Drill

An organization's customer database (high impact if breached) has a 15% likelihood of being targeted this quarter based on threat intelligence. A separate internal wiki (low impact if breached) has a 40% likelihood of being targeted. Which asset represents the higher overall risk, and why?

Answer guidance: Risk = Likelihood × Impact, not likelihood alone. Even though the wiki has a higher likelihood, the customer database's much higher impact (SPII exposure, regulatory consequences) likely makes it the higher overall risk — this is exactly why security teams don't prioritize purely by likelihood.

Lab 1.2 — Asset Classification Practice

Classify each as Public, Internal-Only, Confidential, or Restricted: (1) a press release, (2) unreleased Q3 earnings, (3) the company holiday party schedule, (4) a customer's biometric login data.

Answer key: 1 — Public, 2 — Confidential, 3 — Internal-Only, 4 — Restricted.

Scenario Challenge 1.1 — CSF Tier Assessment

An organization has no documented incident response plan and reacts to security events ad hoc, with no formal risk assessment process. What NIST CSF tier would you estimate, and what would moving to Tier 2 require?

Answer guidance: This describes Tier 1 (passive, limited security controls). Moving to Tier 2 would require documenting basic risk management practices and beginning to formalize (even if not yet fully consistent) incident response procedures.

Interview Preparation

🎤Beginner QuestionINT-401

Q: Explain the risk formula and why security professionals focus mainly on the likelihood side of it.

Ideal answer: "Risk equals likelihood times impact. Impact is largely determined by the asset and business context — it's not something a security team can easily change. Likelihood, on the other hand, is directly influenced by the vulnerabilities we patch and the controls we implement, so that's where day-to-day security work has the most leverage."

🎤Cloud Security QuestionINT-402

Q: A company using a PaaS provider experiences a data breach because they left an admin panel misconfigured. Who's responsible, and why?

Ideal answer: "Under the shared responsibility model, PaaS customers are responsible for securing the applications they build, including configuration choices like admin panel settings. The provider secures the underlying infrastructure they host and maintain — but this specific failure is a customer-side misconfiguration, squarely within the customer's responsibility."

Practice Section

1. Which data state describes a file being actively edited in a document editor?
Correct — data actively being accessed or edited is "data in use."
2. True or False: NIST CSF is a legally mandated regulation.
False — NIST CSF is a voluntary framework, though it can help achieve mandatory regulatory compliance.
3. Which cloud service model gives developers back-end tools to build and deploy their own applications?
Correct — PaaS provides development tools; SaaS is finished applications; IaaS is raw infrastructure.

Revision Notes & Cheat Sheet

⚡30-Second RevisionQR-401

Risk = Likelihood × Impact. Assets classified as Public/Internal-Only/Confidential/Restricted. Data has 3 states: in use, in transit, at rest. Cloud models: SaaS/PaaS/IaaS, governed by the shared responsibility model. Security plans = Policies (rules) → Standards (references) → Procedures (steps). NIST CSF = Core (5 functions) + Tiers (1–4 sophistication) + Profiles (templates).

Module Summary

This module established the "asset" half of the course's title — how organizations identify, classify, and manage what's worth protecting, across every data state and cloud deployment model, structured through the NIST CSF's three components.

Key Takeaways

  • Risk is a calculation (Likelihood × Impact), not just a feeling — and security work mainly targets the likelihood side.
  • Asset classification (Public/Internal-Only/Confidential/Restricted) determines how carefully an asset must be handled.
  • Data in use, in transit, and at rest each require distinct protection strategies.
  • Cloud service models (SaaS/PaaS/IaaS) split security responsibility differently under the shared responsibility model.
  • The NIST CSF structures security work through its Core (5 functions), Tiers (sophistication), and Profiles (templates).

📖DefinitionDEF-406

Principle of least privilege (PoLP): A user is only granted the minimum level of access and authorization required to complete a task or function.

Data owner: The person who decides who can access, edit, use, or destroy their information.

Data custodian: Anyone or anything responsible for the safe handling, transport, and storage of information.

⚖️Compliance Note — Audits vs. AssessmentsCMP-401

A security audit reviews controls/policies/procedures against expectations, typically performed annually, sometimes by external third parties. A security assessment checks how resilient current implementations are against threats, typically performed every 3–6 months by internal staff, often as preparation for the annual audit. They're complementary, not redundant.

Cryptography Fundamentals & Caesar's Cipher

📖DefinitionDEF-408

Cryptography: The practice of converting data into an unreadable form (ciphertext) through encryption, and back into readable form (plaintext) through decryption.

Caesar's Cipher: An ancient method attributed to Julius Caesar, shifting letters forward by a fixed number of spaces (a shift value) to encrypt a message.

Cryptographic key: A mechanism used to decrypt ciphertext — in Caesar's Cipher, this is simply the shift value.

Explain Like I'm Five — The Secret Note

If you and a friend agree "shift every letter forward by 3," then "CAT" becomes "FDW." Anyone who doesn't know the number 3 sees only gibberish, but you can instantly decode it. That's Caesar's Cipher — and its biggest weakness is right there in the example: there are only 26 possible shift values, so a computer can brute-force every single one in an instant.

Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)

📖DefinitionDEF-409

Public Key Infrastructure (PKI): An encryption framework securing the exchange of information online through a two-step process — exchanging encrypted information, then establishing trust via digital certificates.

Digital certificate: A file verifying the identity of a public key holder — a digital ID badge signaling authenticity.

Certificate Authority (CA): A trusted entity that issues and digitally signs digital certificates, verifying the certificate holder's identity.

Symmetric vs. Asymmetric Encryption

📖DefinitionDEF-410

Symmetric encryption: Uses a single secret key for both encryption and decryption — faster, but sender and receiver must both know the same secret key.

Asymmetric encryption: Uses a public/private key pair — the public key encrypts, the private key (kept secret) decrypts.

Approved Encryption Algorithms
AlgorithmTypeKey LengthNotes
Triple DES (3DES)Symmetric192 bitsBlock cipher; being phased out but kept for backwards compatibility
AESSymmetric128 / 192 / 256 bitsMost secure symmetric algorithm today; brute-forcing 128-bit AES could take a modern computer billions of years
RSAAsymmetric1,024 / 2,048 / 4,096 bitsNamed for its three MIT creators; used for highly sensitive data
DSAAsymmetric2,048 bitsNIST standard, often complements RSA in PKI
❗Important — Kerckhoffs's PrincipleIMP-404

A cipher must be provably secure even if every detail of the algorithm — except the private key — is publicly known. Custom, secret, "security through obscurity" encryption schemes have repeatedly been cracked quickly once exposed. AES's inner workings are fully public and it's still unbreakable — that's the standard to aim for.

🗡️Industry Insight — HeartbleedIND-402

In early 2014, OpenSSL (a widely used open-source tool for generating PKI keys) disclosed the Heartbleed vulnerability, exposing sensitive memory data across countless websites and applications. It was patched later that year — a clear demonstration of why using up-to-date cryptographic software matters, regardless of how trusted the tool generally is.

Hashing, Salting & Rainbow Tables

📖DefinitionDEF-411

Hash function: An algorithm producing a one-way code (hash value/digest) that cannot be decrypted — unlike encryption, there is no decryption key.

Non-repudiation: The concept that the authenticity of information cannot be denied — hash functions support this by confirming data integrity.

The SHA Family vs. MD5
FunctionDigest SizeStatus
MD5128-bit (32 characters)Vulnerable to hash collisions; still used by some small/mid businesses despite known weaknesses
SHA-1160-bitNot considered collision-resistant
SHA-224 / 256 / 384 / 512224 to 512-bitConsidered collision-resistant (though not invulnerable to all exploits)
📖DefinitionDEF-412

Hash collision: An instance when different inputs produce the same hash value — a serious vulnerability since hashes are used for authentication.

Rainbow table: A file of pre-generated hash values and their associated plaintext — a "dictionary" attackers use to crack hashed passwords.

Salting: Adding a random string of characters to data before hashing, producing a more unique hash value resilient to rainbow table attacks.

Explain Like I'm Five — The Fingerprint Ink

Imagine two people both write the password "password123." Without salt, their hashed entries look identical — an attacker who cracks one instantly knows the other. Salting is like dipping each person's finger in a slightly different color ink before they stamp it: even with the exact same word, every stamp comes out looking completely different.

The AAA Framework: Authentication, Authorization, Accounting

The Three Factors of Authentication
FactorDescriptionExample
KnowledgeSomething the user knowsPassword, security question
OwnershipSomething the user hasOne-time passcode (OTP) via text/email
CharacteristicSomething the user isFingerprint, facial scan
📖DefinitionDEF-413

Single Sign-On (SSO): Combines several logins into one, reducing password fatigue and the number of access points attackers can target.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Requires two or more independent forms of identity verification.

❗ImportantIMP-405

SSO relies on trusted third parties exchanging encrypted access tokens, using protocols like LDAP (Lightweight Directory Access Protocol, mostly on-premises) and SAML (Security Assertion Markup Language, mostly cloud) — often used together. SSO alone is still a single point of failure; combining it with MFA (a "layered defense") addresses that weakness directly.

📖DefinitionDEF-414

Authorization: Determines the actions or privileges a user is allowed once their identity is confirmed.

Separation of duties: Ensures users don't have permissions that would let them misuse a system alone — divides responsibilities among multiple people.

OAuth: An open-standard authorization protocol sharing designated access between applications using API tokens, without transmitting sensitive credentials over the network.

⚠️WarningWRN-402

HTTP Basic Auth transmits usernames and passwords openly over the network with every request — a significant vulnerability compared to OAuth's token-based approach. Recognizing basic auth in an application should be treated as a security smell worth flagging.

📖DefinitionDEF-415

Session: A sequence of network requests/responses associated with the same user, tracked from login to logout.

Session ID: A unique token identifying a user/device for the duration of a session.

Session hijacking: An attacker steals a user's session cookie/token and impersonates them, without ever needing their username or password.

IAM: MAC, DAC & RBAC

Three IAM Access Control Frameworks
ModelWho Grants AccessTypical Use
Mandatory Access Control (MAC)A central authority/system administrator, strictly need-to-knowMilitary, law enforcement, government agencies
Discretionary Access Control (DAC)The data owner decides access levelsSharing a Google Drive folder as editor/viewer/commenter
Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)Determined by the user's organizational roleMarketing staff can see analytics but not network admin tools
🎯Exam TipEXM-401

MAC is also called "non-discretionary control" — because access is never left to the data owner's personal discretion, unlike DAC. If a scenario mentions a strict chain of command or "need-to-know basis," think MAC; if it mentions a data owner personally sharing access, think DAC.

Hands-On Labs & Practical Exercises

Lab 2.1 — Caesar's Cipher Practice

Encrypt the word "SECURE" using a Caesar's Cipher shift value of 4. Then decrypt "MTIB" using the same shift value.

Answer: "SECURE" with shift 4 → "WIGYVI". "MTIB" decrypted with shift 4 → "ILEX" (shift each letter back 4 positions).

Lab 2.2 — Symmetric vs. Asymmetric Scenario

A website needs to (a) securely verify a user's password during login, and (b) quickly encrypt the rest of a long browsing session. Which encryption type fits each need, and why?

Answer: (a) Asymmetric encryption — small amount of highly sensitive data, prioritizing security over speed. (b) Symmetric encryption — larger volume of ongoing data, prioritizing speed once the secure connection is established. This mirrors how real websites operate in practice.

Scenario Challenge 2.1 — IAM Model Selection

A government agency needs the strictest possible access control for classified files, with no room for individual discretion. Which IAM model fits, and why?

Answer: MAC (Mandatory Access Control) — it's the strictest of the three, granting access only through a central authority on a need-to-know basis, with no discretion left to individual data owners.

Interview Preparation

🎤Intermediate QuestionINT-403

Q: Why is salting necessary even if you're already using a strong hash function like SHA-256?

Ideal answer: "A strong hash function protects against collisions, but it doesn't stop an attacker from using a precomputed rainbow table against common passwords — identical inputs still always produce identical hashes. Salting adds random characters before hashing, so even identical passwords produce completely different hash values, defeating rainbow table attacks regardless of how strong the underlying hash algorithm is."

🎤Security Engineer QuestionINT-404

Q: Explain the difference between authentication and authorization with a real-world analogy.

Ideal answer: "Authentication answers 'who are you' — like showing ID at a building's front desk. Authorization answers 'what are you allowed to do once inside' — like your keycard only opening certain floors. You can be authenticated without being authorized for a specific resource, which is exactly why both steps exist separately in the AAA framework."

Practice Section

1. Which encryption type uses a public key to encrypt and a private key to decrypt?
Correct — asymmetric encryption uses a public/private key pair.
2. True or False: Hash functions can be reversed with the correct decryption key.
False — hash functions are one-way; there is no decryption key, unlike encryption.
3. Which IAM model lets a data owner personally decide who gets editor/viewer access?
Correct — DAC (Discretionary Access Control) leaves access decisions to the data owner.

Revision Notes & Cheat Sheet

⚡30-Second RevisionQR-402

Controls: technical/operational/managerial. Data lifecycle: Collect→Store→Use→Archive→Destroy. GDPR/PCI DSS/HIPAA govern personal/card/health data respectively. Symmetric (1 key, fast) vs. asymmetric (public+private key pair, secure) encryption; PKI combines both with digital certificates/CAs. Hashing is one-way; salting defeats rainbow tables. AAA = Authentication (who), Authorization (what), Accounting (logged activity). IAM models: MAC (central authority), DAC (owner's discretion), RBAC (role-based).

Module Summary

This module covered the technical machinery that actually protects the assets classified in Module 1 — encryption and hashing to keep data unreadable and verifiable, and the AAA/IAM frameworks to control exactly who can touch it and when.

Key Takeaways

  • Security controls fall into three types: technical, operational, and managerial — each addressing a different layer of protection.
  • The data lifecycle (Collect→Store→Use→Archive→Destroy) requires distinct controls at every stage.
  • Symmetric encryption trades security for speed; asymmetric trades speed for security — PKI uses both together.
  • Hashing with salting is the standard for secure password storage, defeating both collision and rainbow table attacks.
  • AAA (authentication/authorization/accounting) and IAM (MAC/DAC/RBAC) are the two major frameworks for managing who can access what.

CVE, CVSS & the OWASP Top 10

📖DefinitionDEF-418

CVE list: An openly accessible dictionary of known vulnerabilities and exposures, created by the MITRE Corporation in 1999.

Vulnerability: A weakness within a system. Exposure: A mistake that can be exploited by threats.

CVE Numbering Authority (CNA): An organization that volunteers to analyze and distribute information on eligible CVEs.

❗ImportantIMP-406

To be assigned a CVE ID, a vulnerability must meet strict criteria: it must be independent of other issues, recognized as a security risk, submitted with supporting evidence, and affect only one codebase. Not every reported bug qualifies.

📖DefinitionDEF-419

Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS): A measurement system calculating a vulnerability's impact and urgency, scored 0–10. Lower scores = low risk; higher scores = critical risk. Maintained via the NIST National Vulnerabilities Database.

🛡️Blue Team TipBT-401

The CVE list identifies vulnerabilities in existing software; the OWASP Top 10 (revisited from Course II) mainly influences new or custom software design. Both matter, but they operate at different points in an application's lifecycle — CVE reactively, OWASP proactively.

Security misconfigurationSecurity settings not properly set or maintained — often default settings left unchanged
Vulnerable and outdated componentsUsing unmaintained open-source libraries/dependencies

🏢Industry Insight — SolarWinds (2020)IND-403

The SolarWinds attack is the canonical example of a software/data integrity failure: hackers injected malicious code into legitimate software updates, which SolarWinds then unknowingly distributed to its own customers — a textbook supply chain attack with cascading downstream effects across the U.S. government and private sector.

📖DefinitionDEF-422

End-of-life (EOL) software: Software the manufacturer no longer supports or patches, even though it may still function. CISA recommends discontinuing EOL software entirely, since it poses an unfixable risk — though replacement cost often keeps organizations running it anyway.

Social Engineering: Stages & Tactics

📖DefinitionDEF-426

Social engineering: A manipulation technique exploiting human error to gain private information, access, or valuables — relying on manipulating people rather than computer systems.

The Four Stages of a Social Engineering Attack
StageWhat Happens
1. PrepareAttacker researches the target, sometimes over months
2. Establish trustBuilding rapport or a credible pretext with the target
3. Use persuasive tacticsExploiting curiosity, generosity, or urgency to extract the desired outcome
4. DisconnectThe attacker exits the interaction, ideally without raising suspicion
🏢Industry Insight — The 2020 Twitter HackIND-404

Attackers called Twitter employees pretending to be from the IT department — a basic social engineering scam — and used it to gain access to internal tools, ultimately taking over high-profile accounts belonging to politicians, celebrities, and entrepreneurs. No sophisticated exploit was needed; a phone call and a false identity were enough.

Named Social Engineering Tactics
TacticDescription
BaitingTempting people into compromising security (e.g., USB baiting)
Quid pro quoTricking someone into believing they'll be rewarded for sharing access/information/money
Tailgating (piggybacking)Unauthorized people following an authorized person into a restricted area
Watering holeCompromising a website frequently visited by a specific target group
🛡️Blue Team Tip — Three Training PillarsBT-402

Effective security awareness training focuses on three habits: Stay alert (watch for spelling errors, verify sender identity), Be cautious (limit what's shared on social media), and Control curiosity (resist clicking things that seem too good to be true). These three habits address nearly every named social engineering tactic at once.

Threat Modeling: Six Steps

📖DefinitionDEF-432

Threat modeling: The process of identifying assets, their vulnerabilities, and how they're exposed to threats — an advanced, comprehensive security activity usually performed by a team.

Threat Modeling — Six Steps Explained
StepAction
1. Define ScopeCreate an inventory of assets, classify them
2. Identify ThreatsDefine potential threat actors (internal/external); build an attack tree mapping threats to assets
3. Characterize the EnvironmentAdopt an attacker's perspective — how do customers, employees, partners, vendors interact with the environment?
4. Analyze ThreatsExamine existing protections, identify gaps, rank threats by risk score
5. Mitigate RiskCreate a defense plan — avoid, transfer, reduce, or accept risk
6. Evaluate FindingsDocument everything, implement fixes, record lessons learned
